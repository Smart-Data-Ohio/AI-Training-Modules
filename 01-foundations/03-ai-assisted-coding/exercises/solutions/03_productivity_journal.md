# Exercise 03: Productivity Journal — Solution

There's no "answer key" for this exercise — it's about your own experience.

## What to Look For in Your Journal

After completing the exercise, review your log and look for these patterns:

### Common Patterns (Most Developers Report These)

**AI was fastest for:**
- Boilerplate code (models, schemas, config)
- Test scaffolding
- Format conversions (e.g., one data structure to another)
- Simple utility functions

**AI was slowest for (or negative value):**
- Debugging issues it introduced
- Tasks requiring knowledge of your specific codebase
- Complex logic where the AI's suggestion was "close but wrong"
- Time spent verifying AI output that you could've written faster yourself

### Red Flags in Your Journal

If you see these patterns, adjust your AI usage:

- **"Modified" appears more than "accepted"** — the AI's code needed significant editing. You might be faster writing from scratch for this type of task.
- **Lots of back-and-forth** — if you're re-prompting 3+ times to get the right output, you're probably past the break-even point.
- **Rejected security-related suggestions** — good instinct. Keep that pattern.
- **Time lost to AI-introduced bugs** — track this carefully. It's the hidden cost that productivity metrics miss.

### What "Good" AI Usage Looks Like

The most effective AI-assisted developers tend to:

1. Use AI for the first draft, then edit heavily (not the other way around)
2. Write the hard parts themselves, use AI for the tedious parts
3. Treat AI suggestions as "code review from a stranger" — read everything
4. Know when to stop prompting and just write the code themselves
5. Use AI for exploration ("how does this library work?") more than generation

## Tracking Over Time

If you keep journaling for 2-3 weeks, you'll develop a personal sense of:

- Which **types of tasks** benefit from AI (and which don't)
- Which **tool** works best for your workflow
- Your own **break-even point** (when does prompting take longer than typing?)
- How your **verification speed** improves with practice
